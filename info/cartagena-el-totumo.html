🌋 El Totumo Vulkaan

Cartagena · Colombia

El Totumo is een van de meest bijzondere en unieke bezienswaardigheden van Colombia — een 15 meter hoge mini-vulkaan gevuld met warme, mineraalrijke modder. In plaats van lava spuwt hij modder uit, en die modder is zo dik en drijfvermogengevend dat je er letterlijk op drijft.

Je klimt een trap op en daalt de krater in — een modderig bassin van ±5 meter diameter waar je langzaam ronddrijft terwijl lokale masseurs je schouders kneden. Daarna wassen in het naburige meer waarbij lokale vrouwen je helpen de modder uit elke plooi te verwijderen. Een onmiskenbaar surreële maar geweldige ervaring.

Enterprising locals have turned some of these geologic wonders into tourist attractions, the most famous of which is the Volcán de Lodo El Totumo , located an hour northwest of Cartagena. For COP $20,000 you’ll climb a rickety staircase to the top of the 50ft-high dirt cone, then descend into its crater and float in a vat of buoyant pudding-like gray sludge. Coastal Hikes & Idyllic Beaches ISOLATED VILLAGES ON PANAMA’S BORDER The seaside towns of Capurganá and Sapzurro are only reachable by prop plane from Medellín or a via a boat journey across the Gulf of Urabá from Necoclí or Turbo. Often a stopover for travelers going to or coming from Panama, these laidback settlements are ideal for hiking, snorkeling and diving the healthiest reefs in the country, wildlife-spotting or just relaxing in a remote tropical location. Walk along the Sendero Ecoturístico between the two villages or you can hire a boat for around COP $15,000 to take you there. Capurganá, the regional maritime hub, tends to be busy with moto-taxis and horse carts filling the streets. If you’re looking for some tranquility, walk along the coastline for about 40 minutes north to La Coquerita , a local swimming hole with three rustic pools – two are freshwater and one is at the waterline and fills with seawater and tropical fish. To the south, you’ll find the glistening Bahía El Aguacate and the Hoyo Soplador , one of the biggest blowholes in South America. Peaceful Sapzurro is the last village before reaching Panama. If you arrive along the footpath from Capurganá, take a short detour to the Cascada de Diana , which is tucked away in the lush, green jungle. You can spend the day on the town beach Playa Bonita , or the more private one at Cabo Tiburón , 15 minutes further along the coast. Sotracor has one direct bus to Necoclí, which makes a stop in Montería. Otherwise take Unitransco, Expreso Brasilia or Rapido Ochoa to Montería and switch to a regional bus to Necoclí or Turbo. If you’re heading east to west, you will have to spend the night before taking a boat across the Gulf of Urabá the next morning. In reverse, it’s possible to get from Capurganá to Cartagena in a single, very long day. Bocachica To get a closer look at Cartagena’s outer fortifications – and a glimpse of how the city’s less fortunate live – take a boat to Bocachica , overlooking the channel of the same name on the southern tip of Isla de Tierra Bomba . With around 12,000 mainly Afro-Colombian inhabitants, Bocachica itself is a ramshackle, relatively impoverished village without running water or medical facilities, and a main street that floods at high tide – garbage lines the shores and muddy streets. The public boats terminate at the village dock (they might stop at several villages along the way), from where it’s a relatively simple hike to two forts – there are no signs however, and it’s a good idea to avail yourself of one of the local guides who will approach you on landing. Guides generally speak Spanish only, but they might be able to rustle up an English-speaker if you ask. The village is generally a safe place to wander, but the guides can be very pushy – insisting on a smaller tip or refusing them altogether may result in some aggressive remonstrations. Fortificaciones de Bocachica Free • 655 0277 Perched high above Bocachica on the Cerro del Horno, the Batería del Ángel San Rafael is a large gun battery built 1769 to 1778, with a line of hefty, rusting cannons on the battlements and stellar views of the surrounding area. The solid brick and stonework has been artfully restored, with the moat also intact. Further along the coast, overlooking a small beach popular with locals, the much more substantial Fuerte de San Fernando was built in stages 1753 to 1779 to better defend Bocachica, which, after a sandbar blocked Bocagrande in 1640, was the only access to the city’s harbour. It’s a remarkable structure, laid out in a typical star design common in the period and riddled with dark vaults and passages fluttering with bats. There are a few signs on doorways, but otherwise no information. This engineering feat included a heavy bronze chain dangled across the entrance, beneath the water, to the Fuerte de San José , built 1714 to 1725 and destroyed by Vernon in 1741 – what you see today was reconstructed in the 1750s (this fort is usually off-limits).
