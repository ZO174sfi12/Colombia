🏰 Castillo de San Felipe de Barajas

Cartagena · Colombia

Het Castillo de San Felipe de Barajas is het grootste en imposantste Spaanse fort van de Nieuwe Wereld — een kolossaal bouwwerk van 17e-18e eeuwse militaire ingenieurskunst. Het fort is gebouwd op de Cerro de San Lázaro en domineer de omgeving volledig.

Bijzonder aan het fort is het ondergrondse netwerk van tunnels en gangen — ontworpen zodat soldaten snel van de ene positie naar de andere konden bewegen zonder blootgesteld te worden aan vijandelijk vuur. De architectuur is zo doordacht dat het fort nooit succesvol is bestormd. Een bezoek van 1-2 uur onthult de fascinerende militaire logica achter het ontwerp.

📗 Lonely Planet Colombia 2023

Start with a tour of the imposing Castillo San Felipe de Barajas. After climbing to the top for views, take a walking tour of the magnificently preserved Old Town. Power up with lunch at Época Espresso Bar.

Escape the afternoon sun at the Museo Historico de Cartagena de Indias, housed in the Palacio de la Inquisición, where bone-chilling implements of torture are displayed.

Climb atop the city walls to watch the sun sink over the horizon at the iconic Café del Mar, then have dinner at the rooftop Mar y Zielo gastrobar. Days to Explore More

After the first day in Cartagena, take a day trip to the Islas del Rosario to bask on white-sand beaches. Return to Cartagena, wander the narrow mural-covered alleyways of bohemian Getsemaní, and stop for dinner at Celele.

The next day, head to San Basilio de Palenque, the first free town for escaping enslaved people in the Americas. A local guide will share the town’s unique language, music and heritage. Sample the sweets called alegrías, meaning ‘joy’ in Spanish.

Continue the happiness back in Cartagena on a chiva party bus. End the evening in a steamy salsa club.

Head to coastal Santa Marta, Cartagena’s less-glamorous older sister. The colonial city’s notable sites include the Quinta de San Pedro Alejandrino, the hacienda where Simón Bolívar died.

After a day in town, embark on the challenging four-day trek to the ancient ruins of Ciudad Perdida. Reward yourself with a couple of days recovering on the tropical beaches of PNN Tayrona.

Head to Riohacha to journey into the Wayúu territory of La Guajira for a few days. Stops include Cabo de la Vela and the enigmatic Punta Gallinas, the most northern spot of continental South America.

It’s a short, safe but hot walk from the walls to the castle via Av Pedro de Heredia (you can’t miss it), otherwise taxis will run you here from the Centro Histórico The largest and most important fort defending Cartagena was Castillo San Felipe de Barajas, a mountain of stone looming just east of the walled city like a Maya pyramid. This incredible feat of engineering was built in phases beginning in 1657, and substantially reinforced in the 1760s. The panoramic views from the top, with the whole city visible, are worth the entry fee alone, but there’s not much else to see other than multi-levelled gun batteries – exploring the dimly lit labyrinth of passages that run beneath the battlements can be fun, but to get more out of the experience it’s best to pay for an audioguide. Alternatively, you can hire one of the guides who hang around at the entrance. Convento de la Popa Cerro de la Popa

Taxis 30–45min from El Centro; don’t walk – robberies have been reported along the zigzagging road up the hill For a real bird’s-eye view of Cartagena, head to the Convento de la Popa (aka Convento de la Candelaria), which, wrote Gabriel García Márquez, “always has seemed on the verge of falling over a precipice”. Lying atop a hill known as El Cerro de la Popa (148m), the restored whitewashed convent chapel, founded by the Augustinians in 1606, is clearly visible from almost anywhere in the city. On February 2, when the city celebrates the day of its patron saint, the Virgin of Candelaria, protector against pirates and the plague, a candlelit procession of pilgrims storms the hill. Bocagrande Buses every few minutes (TransCaribe T103); taxis available The forest of shimmering white skyscrapers south of the Centro Histórico is Bocagrande, a densely populated peninsula jutting into the bay. Home to posh condos and hotels, popular primarily with Colombians, Bocagrande does have a beach, though it’s mediocre at best and the water is usually too dirty to swim in. The scene can be a fun break from all things colonial, with palenqueras, women selling home-made African-style sweets – try the ajonjoli made with coconut – and a plethora of bars, shops and emerald marts.
